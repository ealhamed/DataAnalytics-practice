Mean vs Median

The mean is the sum of all values divided by n. It's sensitive to extremes — one massive corporate account can drag the mean far from the "typical" customer.

The median is the middle value when data is sorted. Exactly 50% of observations fall below it. It resists outliers because it only cares about position, not value.

Rule of thumb: When mean > median → right-skewed. When mean < median → left-skewed. When they're roughly equal → symmetric.

Z-Scores & Normal Probability

A z-score tells you how many standard deviations a value is from the mean. z = (x − μ) / σ.

z = (x − μ) / σ

Once you have the z-score, use the standard normal table (or a calculator) to find the probability of being above or below that value.

z = 0 → you're at the mean (50% above, 50% below). z = 1 → top 15.9%. z = 2 → top 2.3%. z = 3 → top 0.1%. The further from 0, the rarer.

How confident are we in our numbers?

We know the sample mean is £142. But how close is that to the true average across all possible customers?

x̄ = £142 s = £59 n = 200
Question 2A
The standard deviation of customer spend is £59. Does this mean our estimate of the average is also uncertain by about ±£59?
Try it yourself first
SE = s/√n. Try s=59, n=200.
Answer: No. £59 is the spread of individual customers. The uncertainty in the average is much smaller.
The key formula: SE = s / √n = 59 / √200 = £4.17

Individual customers vary by ±£59. But our estimate of the average is precise to about ±£4.17. That's 14× more precise — because averaging 200 people cancels out a lot of noise.

See it in action

FreshCart: s vs SE — the difference matters

Standard Deviation vs Standard Error

s (standard deviation) = how spread out individual data points are around the mean.

SE (standard error) = s/√n = how much the sample mean itself would wobble if you took many different samples. It's always smaller than s because averaging cancels noise.

The #1 exam mistake: using s where SE belongs. They are not interchangeable. s describes individuals; SE describes the mean.

To halve the SE, you need to quadruple n (because of the √n in the denominator). Doubling n only cuts SE by ~30%.

Confidence Intervals

A CI gives a range of plausible values for the true population mean. Take the sample mean, then add and subtract a margin of error.

CI = x̄ ± 2 × (s / √n)

The multiplier for 95% confidence is 2. That's it.

What "95% confident" actually means

It does NOT mean "95% probability the true mean is in this interval." Once calculated, μ is either in there or it isn't.

What it means: if you repeated the study 100 times and built a CI each time, about 95 of those intervals would contain the true mean. The confidence is in the method, not any single interval.

KEY CONCEPT
Why does SE = s/√n work? The Central Limit Theorem.

Even if individual customers' spending is weirdly distributed (skewed, lumpy), the average of a sample will be approximately normal — as long as n is large enough (~30+). And the spread of those sample averages is s/√n.

This is why confidence intervals work: we're exploiting the fact that sample means are better behaved than individual data points.

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

Imagine taking 1,000 different samples of 200 FreshCart customers each and computing the mean of each sample. Those 1,000 means would form a bell curve centered on the true population mean, with spread = σ/√n.

The magic: this works regardless of the shape of the original data. Right-skewed spending data? Doesn't matter. The means are still approximately normal.

This is why n matters so much: √200 ≈ 14.1, so the SE is 14× smaller than σ. At n=800, it would be 28× smaller. More data → the mean estimate gets more precise.

Hypothesis Testing with a CI

Every test starts with two competing statements:

H₀ (null) = the "nothing special" claim. Always has "=". This is what you're trying to disprove.

Hₐ (alternative) = what you want evidence for. Has ≠, <, or >.

Think courtroom: H₀ = "innocent." You only convict (reject H₀) if the evidence is strong enough.

The CI Shortcut

Build a CI, then check: claimed value outside → reject. Inside → fail to reject. This is exactly equivalent to a formal two-tailed test at the same confidence level.

Never "Accept" H₀

You either reject or fail to reject. Saying "accept H₀" is always wrong — not finding evidence against something doesn't prove it's true. It's like saying "I didn't find my keys, therefore they don't exist."

What a p-value means

The p-value asks: "If H₀ were true, how surprising would my data be?" Small p (< 0.05) = very surprising = evidence against H₀. It is NOT the probability that H₀ is true.

Setting up hypotheses: H₀ always has "=". The claim you're testing goes into H₀. Hₐ has ≠ (two-sided) or < / > (one-sided). If the question says "test whether average spend is £160," then H₀: μ = 160.
"Accept H₀" = instant mark loss. Write "fail to reject H₀" or "insufficient evidence to reject." Never "accept." Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.
Chapter 3 summary: Build CI → check if claim is inside. Outside = reject H₀. Inside = fail to reject. p-value = probability of data this extreme IF H₀ were true. Small p = strong evidence against H₀. Never "accept" H₀.

Is the £28 difference between Premium and Standard members statistically significant, or could it be due to chance?

Step 4: |t| = 3.91 > 2 → Reject H₀
There is significant evidence that Premium members spend more than Standard members. The £28/month gap is real, not due to chance.

Two-Sample t-Test

Compares two group averages. Calculate the gap, divide by the combined noise (SE). If the gap is large relative to noise (|t| > 2), it's significant.

t = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / √(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂)
Sample Size & Power

With small n (say 20), CIs are wide and tests are weak — you might miss real effects. The same £28 difference that's significant at n=200 could be invisible at n=20. The effect didn't change — your lens got sharper.

The Regression Equation

True model: Y = β₀ + β₁X + ε (there's always random error).
Fitted model: Ŷ = b₀ + b₁X (our estimate from data).

b₀ (intercept) = predicted Y when X is zero. b₁ (slope) = how much Y changes when X goes up by one unit.

Always include direction, units, and "on average"

A correct interpretation says: "For each one-[unit] increase in X, Y changes by b₁ [units of Y], on average."

Is the Slope Significant?

H₀: β₁ = 0 (no relationship). If |t| > 2 or p < 0.05, the slope is significant — X really predicts Y.

t = b₁ / SE(b₁)

A significant slope with low R² means the relationship is real but weak — a quiet signal in a noisy room.

Prediction Intervals

Point forecast: plug X into the equation. Wrap it in a PI ≈ Ŷ ± 2 × Residual SE for honest uncertainty. The PI is wider than a CI because it accounts for individual scatter around the line.

"Holding All Else Constant"

Each coefficient isolates one predictor's effect, assuming all others stay frozen. Income's coefficient drops from 0.85 (SLR) to 0.62 (MLR) because some of income's effect was running through other variables.

Adjusted R²

Regular R² always goes up when you add variables — even useless ones. Adjusted R² penalises for complexity. If it drops when you add a variable, that variable hurts the model.

Adj R² = 1 − [(1 − R²)(n − 1) / (n − k − 1)]
Dummy Variables

Regression needs numbers. For categories (Premium/Standard), create dummy variables: 0/1 columns. k categories → k−1 dummies. The omitted category is the reference group.

D_Standard = −18.3 means Standard spends £18.30 less than Premium (reference), all else equal.

Multicollinearity & VIF

When predictors are correlated with each other (not with Y), the model can't tell their effects apart. Coefficients become unstable — they may flip signs.

Detecting it — two methods:

1. Correlation matrix: check pairwise |r| between predictors. |r| > 0.8–0.9 = problem.

2. VIF (Variance Inflation Factor): measures how much a predictor's SE is inflated by correlation with other predictors. VIF = 1 means no multicollinearity. VIF > 5 = concerning. VIF > 10 = serious problem.

VIF = 1 / (1 − R²ⱼ) where R²ⱼ = R² from regressing predictor j on all other predictors

FreshCart: Frequency VIF = 9.8, Total_Items VIF = 10.1. Both are near 10 because they share 95% of their variance. All other predictors have VIF ≈ 1 — no issues.

Fix: drop one of the collinear pair. Keep the one that's more actionable or has a stronger theoretical basis. Here: keep Frequency (marketing can influence order frequency), drop Total_Items (it's a consequence of frequency, not a lever).

The Intercept

Always keep the intercept. Never remove it based on its t-value or significance.

Question 6B
Income's coefficient dropped from 0.85 (simple regression) to 0.62 (MLR). Why? And what does "holding all else constant" actually mean?
Try it yourself first
In SLR, income captured total effect including indirect. What happens when those other variables enter?
Why it dropped: In SLR, 0.85 captured income's total effect — including indirect effects via correlated variables (richer people may live closer, order more, etc.).
In MLR: 0.62 is the direct effect of income, after controlling for distance, frequency, and membership. The other variables "absorb" the indirect pathways.
"Holding all else constant": "If two customers have the same distance, frequency, and membership, but one earns £1,000 more — that customer spends £0.62 more/month."
Coefficients shrinking from SLR to MLR is normal — it means the SLR was overestimating the direct effect.
Omitted Variable Bias

In SLR, income's coefficient (0.85) was biased upward because income was "taking credit" for the effects of omitted variables that correlate with income.

Adding controls removes this bias. The MLR coefficient (0.62) is a better estimate of income's true direct effect — though even MLR can't prove causation without an experiment.

Common Exam Traps

The ten most common mistakes people make with these concepts. Worth a read before any test or presentation.

Trap 1: "Half of customers spend below £142" — WRONG. Half spend below the median (£128), not the mean. When mean ≠ median, they test this.
Trap 2: Using s instead of SE in the CI — CI = x̄ ± 2 × SE, where SE = s/√n. If s=59 and n=200, SE=4.17. Using 59 instead of 4.17 gives you a CI that's 14× too wide.
Trap 3: "95% probability the true mean is in the CI" — WRONG. The correct interpretation: "We are 95% confident..." The probability is about the method, not any specific interval.
Trap 4: "Accept H₀" — NEVER say this. Say "fail to reject H₀" or "insufficient evidence to reject." On every exam, this costs marks.
Trap 5: Confusing significance with strength — A significant slope (|t|>2) means the relationship EXISTS. R² tells you how STRONG it is. You can have a highly significant but weak relationship (FreshCart: age → spend is significant but R²=3%).
Trap 6: Removing the intercept — Even if the intercept is not significant, ALWAYS keep it. Removing it forces the line through the origin and distorts every other coefficient.
Trap 7: Ignoring multicollinearity — If two predictors have |r|>0.9 or VIF>5, you MUST mention it. Cite the VIF values. Say what the symptom is (unstable coefficients, inflated SEs), what to do (drop one), and which one to keep and why.
Trap 8: Extrapolation — If asked to predict for values outside the data range, flag that the prediction is unreliable. The linear relationship may not hold at extreme values.
Trap 9: Forgetting "on average" and "holding all else constant" — Every regression interpretation needs "on average." Every MLR interpretation needs "holding all other variables constant." Missing these costs marks.
Trap 10: PI vs CI confusion — PI = for a single new observation (wider). CI = for the mean of all observations at that X (narrower). "Predict for a customer" = PI. "Estimate average spend" = CI.
